# Research and Case Basis

Use this file to support judgment, not to manufacture certainty. No message guarantees a reply, date, or relationship.

## Evidence Tiers

- **Tier A: peer-reviewed or large behavioral data.** Use for general communication mechanisms.
- **Tier B: first-party platform guidance or named Japan-market surveys.** Use for local heuristics, not universal rules.
- **Tier C: community reports and coaching examples.** Use only to identify recurring practical patterns.
- **Tier D: local user cases.** These show wording the user accepted or corrected; they do not prove downstream romantic success unless the user reported it.

Do not quote a response-rate claim unless the source exposes its sample and method. Do not convert an association into a guarantee.

## Conversation Mechanisms

### Responsive follow-up questions

Huang et al. found that people asking more questions, especially follow-up questions, were better liked because they appeared responsive; follow-up questions in speed-dating conversations were associated with more second-date agreement. The practical rule is not "always ask a question." It is "show you heard the answer, then ask one connected question when useful."

Source: https://doi.org/10.1037/pspi0000097

### Reciprocal self-disclosure

Sprecher et al. found that turn-taking reciprocal disclosure produced greater liking, closeness, perceived similarity, and enjoyment than one-sided disclosure. Use matched-depth alternating disclosure rather than interviews or monologues.

Source: https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jesp.2013.03.017

### Responsiveness before performance

Romantic responsiveness research emphasizes understanding, validation, and care when responding to a partner's disclosure. This supports the ordering `emotion/meaning -> response -> next topic`, especially after personal disclosures.

Source: https://doi.org/10.1016/j.copsyc.2023.101652

### Calibrated self-presentation

Kashian et al. found that self-disclosure in computer-mediated communication can increase liking through interpersonal attributions, while disclosure that feels too intimate too soon can create negative attributions.

Source: https://doi.org/10.1016/j.chb.2017.01.041

Choe et al. analyzed a large online-dating message dataset and reported positive associations for mentioning the recipient and calibrated message length, while excessive self-promoting disclosure reduced successful outcomes. Do not translate culture-specific slang findings directly into Japanese; retain the robust operational lesson: orient the message toward her and avoid status-heavy self-promotion.

Source: https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jbusres.2025.115572

### Humor and teasing

Positive humor is generally safer for romantic interest than negative or aggressive humor. Conversation analysis shows that teasing can invite intimacy only when there is a teasable action and the other person can affiliate with or decline the playful frame. Therefore use situational teasing after warmth, never negging or identity attacks.

Sources:

- https://doi.org/10.5964/ejop.v12i3.1105
- https://doi.org/10.1177/1461445617734936
- https://doi.org/10.1007/s42761-023-00190-5

### Effort signals in text

Fang et al. found that texting abbreviations can make senders seem less sincere and reduce willingness to reply because they signal lower effort. Japanese shorthand is culturally different, so do not ban casual language; use the broader lesson that low-effort-looking compression can hurt when rapport is not established.

Source: https://doi.org/10.1037/xge0001684

## Large-Scale Dating Communication

A dataset of about two million mobile-dating conversations found that the majority of reciprocated conversations that exchanged phone numbers did so within the first 20 messages. This is descriptive and platform-specific, but it supports avoiding endless text when mutual interest is already clear.

Source: https://arxiv.org/abs/1607.03320

The BMJ evidence review on moving from online contact to a first date found support for short personalized invitations that address a profile trait. Treat this as broad directional evidence because the underlying studies span older platforms and contexts.

Source: https://ebm.bmj.com/content/20/2/48

## Japan-Market Evidence

### Omiai message survey

A 2023 Omiai/Love-Kon Lab survey of 212 users reported:

- 75% had stopped an exchange because of message content or frequency;
- common reasons included lack of conversational energy, difficult-to-answer content, and slow replies;
- positive impressions were associated with active questions, polite language, and response within 24 hours;
- the accompanying guidance recommends referencing the recipient's profile and making an early, reassuring, time-bounded date invitation.

Operational use:

- make messages answerable;
- be polite without sounding formal;
- respond when practical, without treating speed as an obligation;
- make the first date feel safe and bounded.

Source: https://prtimes.jp/a/?c=25460&f=d25460-66-325554739e61e3157602c6f188fe8d17.pdf&r=66

### Omiai generation survey

A survey of 786 Omiai users reported that Gen Z messages averaged about two lines and fewer than two emoji, with shorter time to meeting than older groups. This is a cohort pattern, not a requirement.

Operational use:

- default to concise messages for users in their twenties;
- match her actual length and style over demographic stereotypes;
- do not assume more weeks of messaging create better compatibility.

Source: https://prtimes.jp/a/?c=25460&f=d25460-61-b04462bb0202125838e7cb843668d42d.pdf&r=61

### with user survey

A 2024 survey of 181 people who had used with reported:

- hobbies and weekend routines were the most common conversation topics;
- men commonly invited after 6-10 exchanges, while women more often preferred 11-15;
- 54% exchanged LINE after messages had accumulated;
- 47% gradually reduced honorific language during messaging.

Operational use:

- treat message counts as ranges, not gates;
- let reciprocity and safety override the clock;
- soften formality gradually rather than abruptly;
- let LINE serve coordination after investment.

Source: https://prtimes.jp/main/html/rd/p/000000034.000040575.html

### First-date survey

A 2024 Zexy Enmusubi survey of roughly 200 users reported an average first date of 110 minutes; cafes and similar public venues were most common. The most cited positive moments included matching conversational tempo, smiling/bright behavior, and feeling listened to.

Operational use:

- plan a short public first meeting;
- prioritize conversational rhythm and listening over impressing;
- do not treat the first date as a performance or interview.

Source: https://prtimes.jp/a/?c=11414&f=d11414-2740-6cfd1008ce12dad4f3dcb68f278a554f.pdf&r=2740

### Platform opener guidance

Bumble's first-party guidance recommends selecting one profile photo or shared interest, asking `what/when/where` or favorite-type questions, and optionally adding one's own preference. This aligns with `one hook + small self-disclosure + easy question`.

Source: https://bumble.com/the-buzz/quick-bumble-opening-line

## Safety

Japan's National Police Agency warns that early migration from matching apps to LINE can be a romance-fraud signal. Translation tools and generative AI can assist impersonation; investment, marriage-cost, money, and account-transfer requests are high-risk.

Source: https://www.npa.go.jp/bureau/safetylife/sos47/case/sns-romance/romance/

Operational rule:

- screen authenticity before optimizing attraction;
- keep suspicious contacts in-app;
- do not click unknown links or transfer money/data;
- recommend block/report when high-risk signals persist.

## Local Case Lessons

These cases were accepted or refined by the user. They validate fit with the user's voice, not romantic outcomes.

1. **Profile-hook opener:** Tokyo food was more natural than stacking moving, games, embroidery, hiking, live music, and eating out.
2. **Concrete routine:** `日中は実験でずっと座っていることが多いので` was preferred over generic fitness motivation.
3. **Language mismatch:** reassurance plus one easy timeline question worked better than making language ability the whole conversation.
4. **Compliment reply:** accept modestly, avoid self-bragging, then reopen the exchange.
5. **Suspicious LINE bait:** risk judgment came before drafting; the fallback stayed in-app.
6. **Membership-expired one-shot:** transparency about the one-message constraint justified a direct LINE request and reduced bait-like appearance.

## Community Pattern Boundary

Community reports repeatedly praise messages that feel specific, natural aloud, and easy to answer, and criticize polished text that sounds generated or interview-like. They also show that personalized openers still fail when baseline attraction, app activity, or availability is absent.

Use this lesson:

- optimize for authenticity and replyability, not cleverness;
- do not overinvest in a cold opener;
- never interpret nonresponse as proof that the wording was wrong.
